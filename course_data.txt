Probability for Business
Definition: Business probability involves the study and application of
probability theory to business situations.
It deals with the likelihood of different outcomes or events
occurring in the business environment.
In the world of probability, we only talk about likelihood, and there is
no such thing as extreme as 100% or 0%.
Focus: The primary focus is on forecasting and estimating the
likelihood of future events based on certain assumptions.
It’s about understanding and quantifying uncertainty or the
random events.

Applications:

The Main Difference Between Probability and
Statistics
Both are very closely related cousins.
Main difference
Probability is more about predicting future events and quantifying
uncertainty.
Intuitive way to understand “how much” an uncertainty would be.
Statistics is more about analyzing past and present data to make
informed business decisions.
We need to understand the past and the present to predict the future.

Study Tips
• Interpretation questions (Q7, Q15) are high-value and commonly missed. Memorize
the standard language: CIs are about repeated sampling;R2 is about proportion of variation
explained.
• Know which distribution to use. z vs.t for means,z always for proportions. Theσknown

• Set up hypotheses carefully . The alternative hypothesis is what you’re trying toprove; the
null takes the complement (including the equality).
• Type I vs. Type II. Type I = rejecting a trueH0 (false alarm). Type II = failing to reject a

What is Data?
Data refers to raw facts, figures, and details collected from various
sources that are used for analysis, decision-making, or understanding
phenomena. Data can take many forms, including numbers, text,
images, and more. It can be structured, like in tables and databases,
or unstructured, like emails or social media posts.
In essence, data is the basic building block from which information is
derived. When processed, interpreted, or analyzed, data becomes
meaningful information that can help answer questions, solve
problems, and guide decision-making.

What is Data?
Data are thefacts and figurescollected, analyzed, and summarized
for presentation and interpretation.
Elements are the entities on which data are collected.
A variable is a characteristic of interest for the elements.
A dataset consists of all the data collected for a particular study.
The total number of data values in a complete data set is the number
of elements multiplied by the number of variables.
An observation is the set ofmeasurements obtained for a particular
element.
A data set with n elements contains n observations.

Correlation vs. Regression
Correlation
Correlation measures the strength and direction of the linear
relationship between two variables.
It is quantified by the correlation coefficient which ranges from -1 to
+1, with 0 indicating no linear relationship.
Correlation is only concerned with the strength of the relationship
between variables;it does not infer causality or dependence.
A high correlation between study hours and test scores does not imply
that studying causes higher scores, as other factors may be involved.
Regression
Regression goes beyond measuring the strength of the relationship and
models the nature of the relationship between a dependent variabley
and one or more independent variablesxi
Regression implies causality when the model assumptions are met, as it
attempts to explain how changes inxi influence y
Example: Using regression, you can predict sales revenuey based on
advertising expenditure x

What Does An IQR of 80 Mean?
The IQR is the range between the first quartile (25th percentile) and
the third quartile (75th percentile) of the dataset.
An IQR of 80 means that the middle 50% of your data is spread out
over a range of 80 units.
Robustness to Outliers: Unlike range, which considers the extreme
values, the IQR focuses on the central part of the dataset and is less
influenced by outliers or extreme values. A higher IQR indicates more
variability among the middle half of your data.
Consistency of Data: If the IQR is small, the middle 50% of your data
points are relatively close to each other, indicating consistency or less
variability. Conversely, an IQR of 80 suggests that there is a considerable
spread among the middle half of your data.

Step 3: Analyze the result
The t-test will provide a t-value and a p-value:

Correlation vs. Regression
Correlation
Measures strength and direction
of linear association.
One number: 𝑟 ∈ [−1, +1].
Symmetric:
corr(𝑥, 𝑦) = corr(𝑦, 𝑥).
Says nothing about causation.
Regression
Models the shape of the
relationship: a line.
Gives an equation: ̂ 𝑦 = 𝛽0 + 𝛽1𝑥.
Asymmetric: 𝑦 on 𝑥 ≠ 𝑥 on 𝑦.
Used for prediction and inference.

Correlation tells you whether two things move together. Regression tells
you how one moves with the other, and lets you predict.

The Four Assumptions: L-I-N-E
OLS gives unbiased estimates and honest 𝑝-values only when the following
hold:
L – Linearity The true relationship between 𝑥 and 𝑦 is a straight line.
I – Independence Observations (residuals) are independent of each other.
N – Normality of residuals Residuals are approximately normally
distributed.
E – Equal variance Residuals have constant spread across all values of 𝑥
(“homoscedasticity”).
Aside: an error is the unobservable deviation from the population line; a residual
is the observable deviation from the fitted line. Residuals are our window into the
errors.

Independent Events
Two events A and B are independent if
P(A|B) = P(A) or P(B|A) = P(B)
Otherwise, the events are dependent if
P(A|B)̸= P(A) or P(B|A)̸= P(B)

More About Independent Events
Definition: Two events are independent if the occurrence of one event
does not affect the probability of the other occurring.
The Scenario: Imagine you are flipping a coin and rolling a standard
6-sided die.
Event A: The coin lands on Heads.
Event B: The die lands on 4.
Why it is Independent: The coin has no memory or connection to the
die. Getting “Heads” does not make it more or less likely that the die
will roll a “4.” The two systems are physically separate.
Ian Liu Department of Fin&BA Trinity University BAT2301 - Statistics for Business 3 / 34
More About Mutually Exclusive Events
Definition: Two events are mutually exclusive if they cannot happen at
the same time. If one happens, the other strictly cannot.
The Scenario: Imagine you are rolling a single die one time.
Event A: You roll a 2.
Event B: You roll a 5.
Why it is Mutually Exclusive: You cannot roll a 2 and a 5 on the same
toss of a single die. The occurrence of Event A makes Event B
impossible for that specific trial. In a Venn diagram, these circles
would never touch (they are disjoint).

A quick note:
Do not confuse the notion of mutually exclusive events with that of
independent events.
Two events with nonzero probabilities cannot be both mutually exclusive
and independent.
If one mutually exclusive event is known to occur, the other cannot
occur.
Thus, the probability of the other event occurring is reduced to 0.

Multiplication Law
The multiplication law is based on the definition of conditional
probability, and it provides a way to compute the probability of the
intersection of two events
P(A∩B) = P(A|B)P(B) or P(B∩A) = P(B|A)P(A)
In the case of independent eventsP(A|B) = P(A) or P(B|A) = P(B),
and the multiplication law for independent events becomes
P(A∩B) = P(A)P(B) or P(B∩A) = P(B)P(A)
Therefore, two eventsA and B are dependent when
P(A∩B)̸= P(A)P(B)

Given an event B, and a set of mutually exclusive and exhaustive
events A1, A2, ..., An, and we know the prior probabilities
P(A1), P(A2), ..., P(An). Then the posterior is:

What Is Binomial Distribution? And When To Use
It?
A binomial distributionis a statistical tool to model the number of
successes in a fixed number of trials (or experiments) where each trial
has only two possible outcomes: success or failure.
Think of it as flipping a coin multiple times and counting how many
heads (successes) you get.
Use the binomial distribution when:
You want to model the number of times an event occurs (like getting
heads) in a fixed number of attempts (like flipping a coin).
You’re dealing with situations where there are only two possible
outcomes (success or failure) for each attempt.
You know the probability of success for each trial and want to find out
the likelihood of getting a specific number of successes.

Binomial Distribution: Used when you have a fixed number of trials,
each with two possible outcomes (success or failure). You’re interested
in the number of successes.
Example: Flipping a coin 10 times and counting how many heads
(successes) you get.
Uniform Distribution: Used when all outcomes are equally likely. Every
possible outcome has the same probability.
Example: Rolling a fair die, where each of the six faces (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, or
6) has an equal chance of showing up.

1 The experiment consists of a sequence ofn identical trials.
2 Two outcomes, success and failure, are possible in each trial.
3 The stationarity assumption: the probability of a success, denoted byp,
and the probability of a failure, denoted by 1−p, do not change from
trial to trial.
4 The trials are independent.

Why Interval Estimation?
You don’t want to give an overly confident answer for something you
are not very sure.
It’s like a way of giving a smart guess with a buffer zone, backed with
statistical evidence, knowing you won’t always be 100% precise, but
you’ll be close enough most of the time.
Imagine you’re launching a new product, and you’re trying to guess how
much profit it will make. An interval estimate is like saying, “We don’t
know exactly, but we estimate the profit will be between $50,000 and
$80,000.”
You are going out with friends and your parents ask you when you are
gonna be home? Say something-ish, not an exact time.

Adequate Sample Size
The sampling distribution of¯x is normal if the underlying population
distribution is normal; otherwise, a sample size ofn≥30 is required.

sample size of less than 15 can be used.
For small sample sizes, a histogram of the sample data should be built
to learn about the shape of the population distribution.

Degrees of Freedom refer to the number of independent pieces of
information (data points) that go in the computation of the sample
standard deviation, s, and with thenth data point being “locked” by
the computations of the sample mean,¯x, needed for the computation
of s.
n−1 is how we determine the degree of freedom
Why n−1?
Imagine you’re at a party with a group of friends, and you’ve decided to
line up for a photo. If the photographer asks everyone to spread out
evenly across the frame, the first few friends can choose their spots
freely, but the last friend’s position is determined by everyone else’s
choice to maintain the balance.
In statistics, this is like havingn−1 degrees of freedom: forn friends,
n−1 can choose their spot freely, while the last spot is constrained by
the overall arrangement,n−1 data points are free to vary, but the last
is determined by the sum needed to meet the mean.
